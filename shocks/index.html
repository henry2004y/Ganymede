Double Adiabatic Theory

The classical approach by Chew, Goldberger, and Low CGL utilizes the MHD framework by assuming isotropic distributions parallel and perpendicular to the magnetic field, which results in scalar pressures on the two sides of the shock.

When we shift to the MHD with anisotropic pressure tensor

Pij=p⊥δij+(p∥−p⊥)BiBj/B2, P_{ij} = p_\perp \delta_{ij} + (p_\parallel - p_\perp)B_i B_j / B^2, Pij​=p⊥​δij​+(p∥​−p⊥​)Bi​Bj​/B2,

where p⊥p_\perpp⊥​ and p∥p_\parallelp∥​ are the pressures perpendicular and parallel w.r.t. the magnetic field, respectively. For the strong magnetic field approximation, the two pressures are related to the plasma density and the magnetic field strength by two adiabatic equations,

ddt(p∥B2ρ3)=0,ddt(p⊥ρB)=0.\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dt}\Big( \frac{p_\parallel B^2}{\rho^3} \Big) &= 0, \\ \frac{d}{dt}\Big( \frac{p_\perp}{\rho B} \Big) &= 0. \end{aligned}dtd​(ρ3p∥​B2​)dtd​(ρBp⊥​​)​=0,=0.​

This is also known as the double adiabatic theory,[5] which is also what many people remember to be the key conclusion from the CGL theory. Here I want to emphasize the meaning of adiabatic again: this assumes zero heat flux. If the system is not adiabatic, the conservation of these two quantities related to the parallel and perpendicular pressure is no longer valid, and additional terms may come into play such as the stochastic heating.

[5] These are constants at a fixed location in time: it is not correct to apply these across the shock!
The general jump conditions for discontinuities in a collisionless anisotropic magnetoplasma in the CGL approximation were derived by Abraham-Shrauner 1967.

Anisotropy in the solar wind

Observationally, Pioneer 6 showed that the ion temperature anisotropy in the solar wind at 1AU generally has T∥>T⊥T_\parallel > T_\perpT∥​>T⊥​[7]. It may possibly be explained by the conservation of the 1st adiabatic invariant Scarf+, 1967[8].

[7] There are 3 interesting discoveries from Pioneer 6 ARC plasma measurements:
1. high fluctuations of flow velocity outside the solar ecliptic plane;

  1. anisotropic ion thermal distribution (T∥/T⊥∼[2,5]T_\parallel / T_\perp \sim [2,5]T∥​/T⊥​∼[2,5]);

  2. presence of a 3rd species, helium, from charge-to-mass ratio analysis of the angular and energy distributions.

The magnetic moment

μ=mv⊥22B \mu = \frac{m v_\perp^2}{2B} μ=2Bmv⊥2​​

is conserved as the collisionless solar wind flows outward from the sun. Near the solar equator the mean field magnitude declines with

Br(r)≃Br(r0)(r0r)2 B_r(r) \simeq B_r(r_0)\Big( \frac{r_0}{r} \Big)^2 Br​(r)≃Br​(r0​)(rr0​​)2

from the Parker spiral solar wind model and Ωr=2.94×10−6\Omega_r = 2.94\times 10^{-6}Ωr​=2.94×10−6 rad/s being the angular frequency of the rotation of the sun.

Earth bow shock

Using data from the AMPTE/IRM spacecraft, Hill+ 1995 have shown that the double adiabatic equations do not hold in the magnetosheath. Moreover, the thermal behaviour of the magnetosheath is studied by Phan+ 1996 using WIND spacecraft data. They report that most parts of the magnetosheath are marginally mirror unstable.[10]

[10] WIND has electron observations and shows Te⊥/Te∥∼1.3T_{e\perp} / T_{e\parallel} \sim 1.3Te⊥​/Te∥​∼1.3 in the magnetosheath.
